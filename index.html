PSSM2 – Navigating the Path to Diagnosis and Management – Part Two

At this time of writing, there is still more research required to have more definitive options for diagnosing and managing. There is currently no cure for this disease.

While there is much debate about the efficacy of genetic testing, and criticism by the purely scientific community, there is ongoing evidence and research, the precursor to pure science, that can give some direction to those seeking answers.

Diagnosis of PSSM2

Diagnosing PSSM2 involves a combination of clinical examination, genetic testing, and muscle biopsy if necessary.

Your veterinarian should conduct a thorough physical examination, paying close attention to gait abnormalities, muscle symmetry, and overall muscle condition.

Genetic testing can help identify whether the horse carries the gene mutation associated with PSSM2, providing valuable information for breeding purposes and informing management strategies.

A muscle biopsy may be recommended to confirm the presence of abnormal polysaccharide accumulation within muscle cells. This procedure involves extracting a small sample of muscle tissue for microscopic analysis.

NOTE: Our research indicates horses have to be actively having issues due to PSSM2 in order to verify results of a muscle biopsy.

Management of PSSM2:

While there is no cure for PSSM2, effective management strategies can help alleviate symptoms and improve the quality of life for affected horses. Here are some key considerations:

  1. Dietary modifications: Nutrition plays a crucial role in managing PSSM2. Horses with PSSM2 benefit from a low-starch, high-fiber diet to minimize the accumulation of glycogen in muscles. Feed options such as beet pulp, alfalfa hay, and high-quality grass hay are recommended. Additionally, supplementation with omega-3 fatty acids, such as flaxseed or fish oil, can help reduce inflammation and support muscle function.
  2. Regular exercise: While it's important to avoid overexertion, horses with PSSM2 benefit from regular, consistent exercise to maintain muscle tone and flexibility. A structured exercise program, tailored to the individual horse's capabilities and limitations, can help prevent muscle stiffness and reduce the risk of tying-up episodes.
  3. Medication and supplements: In some cases, veterinarians may prescribe medications or supplements to manage symptoms associated with PSSM2. This may include muscle relaxants to alleviate muscle stiffness or antioxidants to support muscle health and reduce oxidative stress.
  4. Environmental management: Providing a suitable living environment is essential for horses with PSSM2. Ample turnout time in a spacious paddock or pasture allows for natural movement and grazing, which can help alleviate muscle tension and promote relaxation.
  5. Regular veterinary monitoring: Ongoing veterinary care is crucial for monitoring the progression of PSSM2 and adjusting management strategies as needed. Routine physical examinations, muscle condition scoring, and periodic bloodwork can help track changes in the horse's condition and ensure timely intervention.
